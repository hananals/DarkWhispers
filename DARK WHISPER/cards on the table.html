Mr. Shaitana, a wealthy and enigmatic host with a flair for the dramatic, threw a dinner party like no other. Among his exclusive guest list were four individuals with dark pasts and four of the sharpest minds in crime detection, including the legendary Hercule Poirot.

Shaitana, known for his love of secrets, made a chilling claim over dinner: he enjoyed collecting murderers—those who had gotten away with their crimes. He suggested that among the guests were four individuals who had committed the perfect murder. Poirot, intrigued but cautious, listened intently.

After dinner, Shaitana proposed a game of bridge. The four suspects—Dr. Roberts, a charming physician; Major Despard, a stoic adventurer; Mrs. Lorrimer, an elegant widow; and Miss Anne Meredith, a timid young woman—played at one table. Poirot and the detectives played at another.

The evening took a deadly turn when, after the bridge game ended, Shaitana was found dead, stabbed with a jeweled dagger.

With the suspects confined to the house, Poirot took charge, assisted by Superintendent Battle, Colonel Race, and Ariadne Oliver, a famous (and eccentric) mystery novelist. Poirot declared, “To solve this crime, one must understand the psychology of the murderer. A bridge game reveals much about its players.”

The investigation focused on the four suspects, each with a motive and opportunity to kill Shaitana.

The Suspects:

1. Dr. Roberts
A confident and affable man, Roberts denied any dark deeds. But whispers of a patient’s sudden death and a potential inheritance cast suspicion on him.
2. Major Despard
A man of action, Despard had tales of dangerous expeditions. Could a mysterious death in the jungle link back to him?
3. Mrs. Lorrimer
Calm and composed, Mrs. Lorrimer revealed nothing but skill during the bridge game. Yet, her poise hinted at hidden depths.
4. Anne Meredith
Sweet and unassuming, Anne seemed incapable of violence. But her nervousness and an unexplained poisoning in her past suggested otherwise.

Poirot delved into the psychology of the suspects, using their behavior during the bridge game as a lens. He examined their bids, their strategies, and even their seating arrangements. “A person’s character,” he mused, “is revealed in how they play a game.”

Clues emerged. A fragment of conversation, a discarded letter, and a missing pair of gloves all pointed to a deliberate act of murder. Poirot questioned the suspects, gently coaxing out inconsistencies and unraveling their secrets.

In a dramatic gathering, Poirot pieced together the puzzle. The murderer was Mrs. Lorrimer, who had killed Shaitana out of fear he would expose her past crime. Years ago, she had murdered her husband, and Shaitana’s veiled comments had terrified her into taking drastic action.

However, Poirot unveiled an even darker twist: Anne Meredith had also committed murder in her past and tried to cover her tracks during the investigation. Her guilt, while unrelated to Shaitana’s death, was exposed as Poirot meticulously dismantled her lies.‍

Poirot closed the case with his characteristic brilliance, reflecting on the complexities of human nature. “To know the truth, one must understand the soul,” he declared.

With the culprits brought to justice, the surviving guests and detectives left the house, shaken but in awe of Poirot’s genius.‍
